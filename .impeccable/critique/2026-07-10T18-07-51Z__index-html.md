## Anti-Patterns Verdict

**LLM assessment:** Partial fail. The interface is coherent and functional, but the warm cream background, Inter, restrained tomato-and-sage palette, uniform bordered containers, and repeated small uppercase tracked labels make the visual language resemble a familiar AI-generated warm family app. These labels recur in the header and across Tonight, Shared checklist, Quick planning, Shared grocery list, Home inventory, and Selected recipe. Week then shifts into a dense admin surface that conflicts with the Shared Kitchen Table direction.

The interface avoids more severe tells: there is no glassmorphism, gradient text, oversized card rounding, decorative grid background, sketch illustration, or indiscriminate motion. Real recipe photographs, family notes, source pages, and concrete household vocabulary add genuine specificity.

## Overall Impression

Ryan Family Menu has a solid household model and a genuinely useful Today view. The main opportunity is to make the rest of the app inherit Today's confidence: one clear task at a time, visible reassurance, and predictable positioning. Week is the major emotional and cognitive valley because it combines two editable planning systems; recipe transitions and offline states then undermine trust at exactly the moments users need orientation.

## What's Working

1. **Today is task-first and mobile-capable.** The featured meal, Cook this action, shopping summary, and home inventory summary answer the household's immediate questions with strong hierarchy and large targets.
2. **The language matches household reality.** At Home, pantry/fridge/freezer locations, family tasks, family notes, and shared grocery actions feel concrete rather than software-centric.
3. **Recipe content feels authentic.** Real food photography, scanned source pages, allergy information, cooking metadata, family notes, and EN/ES recipe controls create practical value beyond a generic recipe library.

## Priority Issues

### [P1] Week combines two complete planning systems

**Why it matters:** Seven editable day cards sit above a second editable 42-cell calendar. On mobile the Week view measured roughly 16,348px tall, with intentional horizontal scrolling and more than 150 visible select controls in the DOM. This makes planning repetitive, slow, and intimidating for distracted users.

**Fix:** Make the seven-day plan the primary editor. Reduce the month to compact read-only summaries; selecting one date should open a focused date editor. Label recipe actions by meal, such as Open main and Open salad, instead of repeating Open dinner.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable distill Week planning`

### [P1] Recipe transitions destroy orientation

**Why it matters:** On mobile, selecting a recipe landed inside its Steps section rather than at the title and actions; entering edit mode landed partway into the form. A cook cannot immediately confirm which recipe is open, while an editor cannot reliably see how to save or escape.

**Fix:** Focus and scroll to the detail heading when a recipe opens. When editing, preserve the recipe title and keep Save and Cancel reachable with a compact sticky action region. Restore focus to the originating recipe card when detail closes.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable adapt recipe detail and editing`

### [P1] Bilingual and programmatic accessibility are incomplete

**Why it matters:** Spanish copy contains missing accents, some accessible names remain English, and the document language remains English after the toggle. Browser evidence also found the Today task field and grocery textarea relying on placeholder text for accessible names, plus an unlabeled receipt file input. Week includes many 36-40px controls, below the 44px mobile target documented for the product.

**Fix:** Correct Spanish orthography, localize every title and accessible name, update the document language dynamically, add explicit programmatic labels, establish a visible `:focus-visible` system, and bring actionable mobile controls to at least 44px.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable harden bilingual accessibility`

### [P1] Offline states diagnose failure but do not support recovery

**Why it matters:** Groceries and At Home report that saving failed, but offer no Retry action, queued-save status, last-synced time, or explanation that local data remains safe. This directly undermines the shared-first promise because caretakers cannot trust that another person will see their changes.

**Fix:** Distinguish saved locally, queued for sync, syncing, synced, and failed states. Add Retry, last-sync metadata, and reconnection feedback while preserving edits.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable harden offline and synchronization states`

### [P2] The visual identity drifts toward a generic warm dashboard

**Why it matters:** Repeated uppercase labels, cream surfaces, Inter, bordered panels, and identical control geometry flatten the app's personality. Week feels especially corporate even though the product explicitly rejects stiff admin tools.

**Fix:** Reduce uppercase label repetition, let real food and household activity carry more identity, vary section cadence, and remove containers where spacing and typographic grouping provide enough structure.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable bolder app-wide visual identity`

## Persona Red Flags

**Sam, accessibility-dependent:** Meaningful recipe source photos have empty alternatives. Spanish mode leaves English accessible labels and does not update the document language. Some fields rely on placeholders rather than explicit labels, focus styling is left to browser defaults, and Week creates an extremely long linear traversal with many undersized controls.

**Casey, distracted mobile user:** The fixed bottom navigation and its approximately 48px targets work well. Week nevertheless requires both horizontal navigation and extreme vertical movement. Recipe opening and editing can reposition Casey deep inside content, while Add offers no draft or interruption reassurance.

**Jordan, first-timer:** The recipe count format such as 12/12 is unexplained, Add does not reveal that it means adding recipes, duplicate Open dinner controls do not identify their targets, and empty grocery/inventory states describe absence without teaching the first useful action.

**Marta, household caretaker:** Shared tasks and assignees fit her job, but there is no visible author, last-updated time, pending-sync state, or change history. Marta cannot tell whether another household member has current information or whether an offline update will reach them.
